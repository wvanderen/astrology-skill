# Source Canon and Rights Ledger

Use this ledger before enriching doctrine-heavy reference modules. It records
which source families are safe to consult, which editions can be quoted or
excerpted, and where modern translations or commentaries must be treated as
copyrighted research aids only.

## Rights Baseline

- This is a working research ledger, not legal advice.
- Ancient, medieval, and early modern source texts are public domain as texts,
  but modern translations, introductions, notes, critical apparatus, indexes,
  typesetting, and scans can have separate rights.
- As of June 19, 2026, ordinary U.S. copyright term practice places works
  published in 1930 or earlier in the public domain, subject to edge cases such
  as unpublished material, sound recordings, restored foreign copyrights, and
  later revised editions.
- In life-plus-70 jurisdictions, author death date also matters. Alan Leo died
  in 1917, so his own early works are out of copyright in those jurisdictions;
  Vivian Robson died in 1942, so the 1929 Leo/Robson dictionary also appears
  out of copyright in life-plus-70 jurisdictions as of 2013.
- For bundled skill modules, prefer public-domain editions, paraphrase doctrine,
  cite the source family, and avoid copying modern commentary unless the license
  is explicit.

## Priority Source Families

| Source family | Rights posture | Strongest use | Main caution |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Hellenistic theoretical canon: Ptolemy | Ancient Greek text public domain; use public-domain translations where verified | Planets, signs, aspects, dignities, natal topics, timing theory | Ptolemy is systematic and selective, not a complete record of working practice |
| Hellenistic practical canon: Valens | Ancient Greek text public domain; modern translations and critical editions usually copyrighted | Practical delineation, lots, timing, planetary combinations, case examples | Most accessible English material is modern and must not be copied |
| Late antique compendia: Firmicus, Paulus, Rhetorius | Ancient/late antique source texts public domain; modern translations copyrighted | Houses, planets, delineation recipes, doctrine preservation | Often fatalistic, medicalized, and socially dated |
| Medieval Arabic and Latin: Masha'allah, Sahl, Abu Ma'shar, Bonatti | Original and early printed Latin sources public domain; modern English translations copyrighted | Reception, questions, elections, revolutions, timing, medieval transmission | Transmission layers are complex; do not flatten Arabic, Latin, and modern reconstruction into one voice |
| Early modern English: Lilly | 1647 and 1659 editions public domain | Horary, houses, significators, reception, aspects, electional and natal method | Strongly judgmental seventeenth-century social assumptions |
| Public-domain modern: Alan Leo, Raphael/Cross, Sepharial | Verify edition by publication date; modern reprints are copyrighted | Psychological, character, synthesis, progressions, early modern-English style | Theosophical, fatalistic, colonial, gendered, or moralizing language needs adaptation |

## Extraction Rules for Future Tasks

- For classical doctrine modules, start with Ptolemy for theoretical structure,
  Valens for practical Hellenistic use, then cross-check with late antique or
  medieval sources when the module needs houses, lots, timing, questions, or
  elections.
- For horary modules, use Lilly as the public-domain English anchor, then add
  Bonatti, Masha'allah, Sahl, or related medieval sources only when the edition
  and translation rights are clear.
- For public-domain modern psychological language, prefer Alan Leo editions
  published during his lifetime or otherwise no later than 1930. Do not use
  modern reprint introductions or reset editorial notes as source text.
- When a module introduces doctrine from a copyrighted modern translation,
  paraphrase at a high level and cite the older source family. Do not import
  distinctive modern phrasing, tables, examples, or commentary.
- When a source contains medical, legal, financial, sexual, racial, classed,
  gendered, or deterministic claims, record the doctrine only as historical
  context and adapt any usable interpretive principle to the skill's existing
  ethics and uncertainty guardrails.
