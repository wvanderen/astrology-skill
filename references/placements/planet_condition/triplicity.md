# Triplicity

Use this module when a planet is explicitly supplied as having triplicity
dignity or support by triplicity ruler.

## Core significations

- Triplicity describes support from the elemental team or environment of the
  sign.
- The planet may have access to allies, context, shared resources, or a familiar
  operating climate even when it does not rule the sign.
- Triplicity is a supportive condition, not proof that the planet's outcomes are
  automatically easy, ethical, or uncomplicated.

## Interpretive effect

- Emphasize cooperation, environmental fit, backup, and the ability to draw on
  compatible resources.
- Triplicity can stabilize a planet that otherwise has mixed condition,
  especially when the relevant triplicity ruler is also well placed or
  configured.
- The support may be social, contextual, procedural, or circumstantial rather
  than fully self-directed.

## Classical notes

- Triplicity is elemental-team support: the planet draws on allies and a
  familiar climate even without ruling the sign.
- Ptolemy uses a two-lord day-and-night triplicity system (a day ruler and a
  night ruler per triangle); the later three-lord Dorothean system, with a
  participating lord, is a separate tradition. Name the system the supplied data
  implies and do not mix them.
- Do not assert a triplicity ruler unless the source data names one. State the
  day/night context only when sect is supplied.

## Synthesis instructions

Modify interpretation by:

- Sect: use the supplied day, night, or participating triplicity ruler when the
  source data distinguishes them.
- House placement: describe where the planet has contextual support or allies.
- Ruler condition: assess the named triplicity ruler when supplied; do not
  derive missing triplicity rulers from tables unless the task includes them.
- Other dignity or debility: triplicity can mitigate detriment, fall, cadency,
  or difficult aspects, but it does not erase them.

## Source notes

- Triplicity doctrine paraphrases Ptolemy, *Tetrabiblos* Book I §18 (Robbins
  Loeb, LacusCurtius; public domain in the U.S. via non-renewal); the three-lord
  Dorothean system is noted as a later medieval/Hellenistic tradition. See
  `references/classical_doctrine_notes.md` for the source pass and provenance
  tags; doctrine is paraphrased, not quoted, and no triplicity scheme is asserted
  unless the supplied data names one.
